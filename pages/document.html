What is Partimento?

Partimento is a practice method used by musicians in the 18th century, where they would create additional voices and harmonies upon a simple musical framework, resulting in a more elaborate and engaging melody.

What you should have learned first

  • Other basic skills related to reading standard musical notation.

Step.2 : Beginner

Rule of Octave

Basses often move stepwise, ascending or descending by one note. This stepwise motion was so common that one of the first lessons for musicians in the 18th and 19th centuries was the Rule of the Octave. This rule teaches how to harmonize an ascending or descending bass line.

Ascending

In any key, the 1st, 5th, and 8th degrees of the scale are considered the most stable, and are harmonized with 3/5. The other degrees (2nd, 3rd, 4th, 6th, and 7th) are less stable and are harmonized with 3/6.

Step.3 Advance

Voice Leading

When playing Partimento, we often use the third position for the right hand, where the root of the chord is on the left. While this approach is common, it can occasionally result in awkward leaps between chords.

For example, in C major, moving from the 1st chord C to the 2nd chord D causes the highest note to jump from G to B. This kind of jump doesn't always sound smooth. However, by using the first position of the 1st chord, the highest note now moves from C to B. This not only minimizes hand movement but also makes the melody smoother and more connected. This technique is called Voice Leading.

Scale Step

The Rule of the Octave covers stepwise motion in both ascending and descending bass lines. However, actual music often involves leaps between scale degrees, such as from I to IV or from vi to iii. To maintain the harmonic tension and resolve towards a stable chord, the Scale Step rules help guide how to harmonize such movements.

I, II, and III Scale Degrees

  • I is the most stable degree and forms the foundation of the piece. Any transition to I should sound resolved and stable, use 3/5 .
  • II introduces a 4 to the usual 3/6 chord.
  • III follows the Rule of the Octave, use 3/6 to create instability.

V Scale Degree

Generally, the V chord is stable and harmonized with 3/5. When moving from V to I or vi, a 7th is added, creating the classical cadential 7th.

IV Scale Degree

Transitions involving the IV scale degree differ when connecting to V. Moving from V to IV uses a 2/4/6 chord, while moving from IV to V uses the typical 3/5/6.

If IV is not connecting to V, use 3/5.

VI Scale Degree

When moving from VI to V, use 3/4 and the augmented 6th. This approach helps with smoother voice leading, similar to how II resolves to I. When moving from VI to VII, use 3/6; otherwise, stick to 3/5.

VII Scale Degree

Generally, the VII degree is harmonized with 3/6.

When VII resolves to I, use 3/-5/6, which sounds similar to a V-I cadence.
